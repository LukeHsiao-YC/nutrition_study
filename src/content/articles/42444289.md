### 重點摘要
鑑於含糖飲料（SSB）與青少年肥胖和代謝功能障礙相關而100%果汁的影響仍有爭議，本研究比較了593名年輕人攝取SSB與100%果汁的代謝組學特徵。研究分析了飲食頻率問卷和非靶向血清代謝物，發現有40種代謝物與SSB攝取相關，39種與果汁攝取相關。結果顯示，兩者僅有3種代謝物共同相關，其餘均為獨特。這表明SSB和100%果汁攝取與大部分不同的代謝組學特徵相關，暗示兩種飲料在年輕人體內可能產生飲料特異性的代謝影響。

### 研究縫隙 (Research Gap)
這項研究區分了含糖飲料與100%果汁在年輕族群中可能誘發的獨特代謝組學特徵，填補了對於這兩種含糖飲品潛在獨特代謝影響的知識空白。然而，未來需要更多研究來重複這些發現，深入探索共同的內源性代謝途徑，並闡明其具體的生理機制。

### 原文資訊 (Original English)
**Title:** Metabolomic Profiles Associated With Sugary Beverage Intakes in Youth.
**Abstract:** Sugar-sweetened beverages (SSBs) have been linked to obesity and metabolic dysfunction in youth; whereas the effects of 100% fruit juice, which is also high in free sugar, remain debated. To compare metabolomic profiles of SSB versus 100% fruit juice intake across childhood and adolescence. Data were from 593 youth in the EPOCH cohort collected during childhood (6-14 years) and adolescence (12-19 years). A food frequency questionnaire assessed total-energy-adjusted SSB and fruit juice intakes over the past week and fasting serum samples were assayed by untargeted metabolomics profiling. Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO) regression with bootstrapping identified metabolites associated with each beverage at each visit. Metabolites consistently selected across visits were retained and compared. Mean (SD) SSB intake was 6.9 (4.9) and 5.2 (3.7) servings/week in childhood and adolescence, respectively, and mean fruit juice intake was 2.4 (2.6) and 1.7 (2.0) servings/week. Of 767 metabolites, 40 were associated with SSB intake and 39 were associated with fruit juice intake at both visits. Only three metabolites were associated with SSB and fruit juice intake (argininate, cortisone, quinate); all others were unique. Both beverages were linked to metabolites of amino acid, xenobiotic, lipid, and carbohydrate metabolism (10, 11, 8 and 1 metabolites, respectively, for SSB; 7, 8, 8 and 3 metabolites, respectively, for juice intake). SSB and 100% fruit juice intake are associated with largely distinct metabolomic profiles, suggesting some degree of beverage-specific metabolic effects in youth. Future studies are needed to replicate these findings, explore the role of common endogenous pathways and characterise mechanisms.
