### 重點摘要
本文報告一名16個月大男童因嚴重左心室功能不全及心因性休克入院，診斷為維生素D缺乏性佝僂病導致的擴張性心肌病變。儘管積極補充維生素D和鈣，患者仍需使用葉克膜（VA ECMO）並最終轉換至左心室輔助裝置（LVAD）以支持心臟功能。患者在LVAD支持95天後心臟功能恢復正常並成功移除裝置，一年後追蹤心臟功能維持正常。此案例突顯了佝僂病是兒童擴張性心肌病變一種罕見但可逆的原因，並展示了LVAD在治療此類病例中的應用。

### 研究縫隙 (Research Gap)
這份報告填補了對於維生素D缺乏性佝僂病導致兒童擴張性心肌病變這種罕見且可能致命情況的治療經驗空白，特別是成功應用左心室輔助裝置作為恢復橋樑的臨床實例。然而，作為單一病例報告，其結果的普遍性有限，未來的研究可能需要探討此類病例的早期篩查策略及更廣泛的治療效果評估。

### 原文資訊 (Original English)
**Title:** Cardiogenic Shock in a Toddler With Rickets.
**Abstract:** Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM) in pediatric patients is most commonly of idiopathic, genetic, or infectious etiology, but rare cases are reported secondary to vitamin D-deficient rickets. Mild rickets is typically associated with skeletal deformities, delayed gross motor milestones, and growth failure, while severe presentations may involve hypocalcemic seizures or DCM. We report a 16-month-old male patient presenting with lethargy, tachypnea, hypotension, and hepatomegaly who was found to have severe left ventricular dysfunction and associated cardiogenic shock. Initial management included intubation, inotropic support, and cannulation to venoarterial extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (VA ECMO). He had hypocalcemia on presentation, laboratory studies demonstrating a low vitamin D level, and radiograph findings of rachitic rosary, splaying of distal metaphyses, and generalized demineralization leading to a diagnosis of vitamin D-deficient rickets. Despite aggressive vitamin D and calcium repletion, the patient was unable to wean from VA ECMO, ultimately requiring transition to a Berlin Heart EXCOR pediatric left ventricular assist device (LVAD) on day 20 of hospitalization. The patient's hospital course was complicated by an LVAD-associated thromboembolic stroke with mild residual neurologic deficits after an unsuccessful mechanical thrombectomy. His left ventricular function gradually normalized, leading to successful explantation after 95 total days of mechanical support. The patient's left ventricular function remained normal at 1 year follow-up. This case highlights a rare but reversible cause of pediatric DCM and includes the use of LVAD support as a bridge to recovery in rickets-associated DCM.
